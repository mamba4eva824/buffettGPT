# Your "Diversified" Index Fund Has a Dirty Secret: 7 Stocks Are Running the Show

*An analysis of S&P 500 concentration using 5 years of financial data on ~500 companies. This is for informational and educational purposes only, not financial advice.*

---

You probably did the responsible thing. You listened to the advice. You set up your 401(k), picked a target-date fund or an S&P 500 index fund, and turned on auto-contributions. Maybe you even felt good about it — 500 companies, nice and diversified, the bedrock of American capitalism working for you in the background.

Here's what nobody told you: you're not really betting on 500 companies anymore. You're increasingly betting on seven.

I pulled five years of financial data on every company in the S&P 500 — daily stock prices, quarterly earnings, revenue, free cash flow — and what I found should make every passive investor stop and think about what "diversification" actually means in 2026. The good news: once you understand what's inside your index fund, there are straightforward ways to manage the risk.

## The Seven Companies That Ate the Index

The "Magnificent 7" — Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet (Google), Meta (Facebook), NVIDIA, and Tesla — now make up roughly **35% of the entire S&P 500 by market value**. That means if you own a standard S&P 500 index fund, more than a third of your money is sitting in just seven companies. (A note for the sharp-eyed: Broadcom has actually surpassed Tesla in market cap, making it the 7th largest stock by size. But "Magnificent 7" has stuck as the label for this group, and these seven stocks still collectively dominate the index.)

Five years ago, these same companies were about 20% of the index. Their share has nearly doubled.

And the top 10 stocks? They're **42.5% of the index** now, up from 30% five years ago. Think about that — 10 companies out of 500 make up more than two-fifths of the whole thing.

## The $100 Test

Here's the simplest way to see what's happened.

If you had invested **$100 in an S&P 500 index fund** at the start of 2021, you'd have about **$186 today**. That's solid — an 87% return over five years.

But that $186 is an average that hides two very different stories:

- **$100 in just the Mag 7** (split equally) would be worth **$323** — more than tripling your money.
- **$100 in the other 490 stocks** (without the Mag 7) would be worth **$175** — barely keeping pace with the overall index.

The Mag 7 returned **+223%**. Everything else returned **+75%**. Same index. Two completely different experiences happening inside of it.

(One caveat: these are price returns, not including dividends. If you reinvested dividends, the gap narrows slightly — the "other 490" tend to pay higher dividend yields than the growth-heavy Mag 7. But the directional story doesn't change.)

## Why "Average" Is Misleading

Here's a number that really drives the point home.

In 2024, the S&P 500 gained 24%. Sounds like a great year for stocks. But when you look under the hood, **only 28% of stocks in the index actually beat the index that year**. The median stock — the one right in the middle if you lined up all 500 — gained just 9.6%.

That means if you had picked a stock at random out of the S&P 500, you had less than a 1-in-3 chance of beating the index. The "average" was being pulled up by a small group of massive winners at the top.

This wasn't a one-year fluke. In 2023, only 30% of stocks beat the index. In 2025, only 31%.

| Year | S&P 500 Return | Stocks That Beat It | Median Stock Return |
|------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **2021** | **+28.8%** | **49%** | **+28.1%** |
| 2022 | -19.9% | 61% | -13.2% |
| 2023 | +24.8% | 30% | +10.1% |
| **2024** | **+24.0%** | **28%** | **+9.6%** |
| 2025 | +16.6% | 31% | +6.0% |

Notice the pattern: even in 2021, a strong market year, barely half of stocks kept pace with the index. 2022 was the outlier — a down year where the majority of stocks actually beat the index (because the biggest stocks fell the hardest). But starting in 2023, when the AI boom kicked in, the index became a two-tier system. A handful of AI-adjacent mega-caps surged, and the rest of the index got left behind.

## The NVIDIA Problem

If there's one stock that illustrates the concentration story, it's NVIDIA.

Five years ago, NVIDIA was about 1% of the S&P 500 — a mid-sized chip company most casual investors had never heard of. Today it's the **single largest company in the index at 8.1%**, worth $4.7 trillion. Its stock is up **+1,159% over five years**.

Here's what that means for your index fund: I estimated NVIDIA's contribution to the S&P 500's total return year by year, accounting for its growing weight in the index.

| Year | NVIDIA's Return | Its Share of the S&P 500 | What It Added to the Index |
|------|----------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| 2021 | +124% | ~1.0% | +1.3 points |
| 2022 | -52% | ~1.8% | -0.9 points |
| 2023 | +246% | ~1.1% | +2.6 points |
| **2024** | **+179%** | **~3.0%** | **+5.3 points** |
| 2025 | +35% | ~6.7% | +2.3 points |

Add it up, and one single company — one out of 500 — was responsible for roughly **12% of the entire S&P 500's gains** over the past five years. Without NVIDIA, the index's 87% return drops to about 76%.

One stock. Twelve cents of every dollar you made.

## The Two S&P 500s

There's a straightforward way to measure whether an index is being driven by its biggest members or by broad-based growth: compare the regular (cap-weighted) S&P 500 to an equal-weight version where every company counts the same.

When the regular version beats the equal-weight version, it means big stocks are outperforming small ones. When equal-weight wins, it means the little guys are doing relatively better.

| Year | S&P 500 (Big Stocks Count More) | Equal-Weight (Everyone Counts the Same) | Difference |
|------|--------------------------------|----------------------------------------|-----------|
| 2021 | +28.8% | +30.6% | Roughly even |
| 2022 | -19.9% | -10.7% | Small stocks winning |
| 2023 | +24.8% | +17.1% | Big stocks winning |
| **2024** | **+24.0%** | **+12.9%** | **Big stocks winning** |
| 2025 | +16.6% | +9.5% | Big stocks winning |

In 2022, you would have been better off in an equal-weight fund (the biggest stocks fell the hardest). Since 2023, the mega-caps have dominated. Over the full five years, the cap-weighted index beat equal-weight by about 18 percentage points. That gap is the "concentration premium" — the extra return you got specifically because NVIDIA, Apple, and friends had outsized influence on the index.

## Where the Money Actually Went

The S&P 500 added about **$25.6 trillion** in market value over the past five years. Where did it go?

The Technology sector alone accounted for **$14.9 trillion of that — 58% of all gains**. The Magnificent 7 specifically contributed **$12 trillion — 47%** of the total. Seven companies. Nearly half of all the wealth created by the 500 largest public companies in America.

NVIDIA alone added **$4.3 trillion** in market cap. To put that in perspective, that's more than the *entire* Energy, Utilities, Real Estate, Basic Materials, and Communication Services sectors *combined*.

Meanwhile, some sectors barely moved. Healthcare stocks had a median return of just 5% over five years. Real Estate: 6.5%. Consumer Defensive: 2.1%. If your retirement fund happened to be overweight in those sectors, the past five years felt nothing like the headline numbers.

## The Sector Most People Don't Talk About

Here's a surprise: the best-performing sector over the past five years wasn't Technology. It was **Energy**, with a median stock return of +110.5%.

But here's the catch — Energy is only 3.6% of the index. So even though energy stocks doubled on average, their small weight meant they barely moved the needle for index fund investors. Tech's median return was a more modest +46%, but because Tech is 45% of the index, it overwhelmed everything else.

This is the math of concentration at work. A sector can dramatically outperform, but if it's a tiny slice of the pie, your index fund barely notices.

## The 2026 Plot Twist

Everything I've described so far — the Mag 7 dominance, the tech-driven concentration, the narrow market — that was the story of 2023, 2024, and 2025. Early 2026 is telling a different story.

In the first weeks of 2026, the sectors leading the S&P 500 look nothing like the ones that dominated the last three years:

| Sector | 2026 YTD | For Context: 5-Year Return |
|--------|---------|---------------------------|
| Energy | **+18.5%** | +110.5% |
| Basic Materials | **+15.8%** | +47.2% |
| Industrials | **+11.7%** | +60.3% |
| Consumer Defensive | +10.1% | +2.1% |
| ... | | |
| Communication Services | -2.8% | +18.3% |
| **Technology** | **-3.6%** | +46.1% |

Read that bottom line again. Technology — the sector that powered 58% of all S&P 500 gains over the past five years, the sector that now makes up 45% of the index — is *the worst-performing sector in 2026*. Dead last.

And it's not just the sector-level numbers. The Magnificent 7 are down **5.8%** in 2026, while the other 490 stocks are *up* **5.4%**. That's an 11-percentage-point gap — and for the first time in three years, it's the rest of the index that's winning.

### What's leading instead

The three sectors at the top of the 2026 leaderboard — Basic Materials, Energy, and Industrials — are "real economy" sectors. Mining companies. Oil producers. Manufacturers. The kinds of businesses that make physical things and pull resources out of the ground.

Over the past five years, these sectors have quietly built strong track records. Energy's median stock more than doubled (+110.5%). Industrials returned +60.3%. But because they're small slices of the index — Energy is 3.6%, Basic Materials is 1.8%, Industrials is 7.7% — their strength barely registered in the headline S&P 500 number that was being dragged around by Apple and NVIDIA.

In total market cap terms over five years, these three sectors added $3.5 trillion combined — Energy ($1.2T), Industrials ($1.9T), and Basic Materials ($331B). That's impressive growth, but it's barely a quarter of what Technology added alone ($14.9T). Their index weight hasn't grown much either: Energy went from 2.8% to 3.6%, Industrials held steady around 8%, and Basic Materials actually *shrank* from 2.3% to 1.8% — because Tech's growth diluted everyone else's share of the pie.

### Is this a rotation?

Wall Street has a term for what might be happening: **sector rotation**. It's when money flows out of one part of the market and into another — typically from sectors that have run up significantly to sectors that look relatively cheaper or have improving fundamentals.

The early signs are there. Tech is decelerating — its median stock has been flat-to-down for a full year (1-year median return: -3.4%). Meanwhile, the sectors now leading have had steady multi-year momentum that's continuing. And Consumer Defensive stocks — traditionally a "safety" play — are surging (+10.1% YTD after a negative year), which often signals that investors are getting cautious.

But it's worth being honest about what we can and can't say from a few weeks of data. A similar rotation happened in early 2022, when tech sold off and energy surged — and then tech came roaring back in 2023. Short-term sector moves don't always become long-term trends.

What *is* clear from the data: after three years of an increasingly narrow market driven by a handful of mega-cap tech stocks, early 2026 is the first period where the rest of the index is meaningfully outperforming. Whether that's a temporary breather or the beginning of a broader shift is the most important question for your index fund right now.

## What the Earnings Say

You might wonder: is this concentration justified? Are these companies actually earning enough to justify their dominance?

Partially. The top 10 stocks command **42.5% of the index's market cap** but earn **33% of its net income** and generate **27% of its free cash flow**. They take in just **18% of total revenue**.

In other words, the market is paying a premium for these companies — betting that their current earnings are just a fraction of what they'll earn in the future. You can see this in their valuations: the mega-cap tech stocks trade at roughly 30x expected future earnings, compared to about 18x for the rest of the index. That premium reflects the market's belief that AI will turbocharge these businesses. It might be a perfectly reasonable bet. Or it might be the kind of thing that looks obvious in retrospect if it works and wildly overoptimistic if it doesn't. That valuation gap is also part of why the 2026 rotation is happening — investors are starting to wonder if the premium has gotten too rich.

## So What Does This Mean for You?

If you're a passive investor in an S&P 500 index fund — and to be clear, I still think index investing is one of the smartest things an ordinary person can do — here's what's worth understanding:

**You are more concentrated than you think.** The label says "500 companies." The reality is that a third of your portfolio is in seven stocks, and 42% is in ten. If NVIDIA or Apple has a bad year, you'll feel it more than you might expect from a "diversified" fund.

**The index is making a bet on your behalf.** Cap-weighted index funds inherently tilt toward winners — as a stock goes up, it becomes a bigger part of the index, so you automatically own more of it. This is a feature in bull markets (you ride the winners) and a risk in reversals (you're overexposed at the top).

**The last three years are not normal.** Having 70% of stocks underperform the index is unusual. It reflects a market driven by a specific narrative (AI) benefiting a specific set of companies. These things can persist for years. They can also unwind.

**Diversification still works — it just looks different now.** If concentration concerns you, you don't have to abandon index investing. Some people add exposure to equal-weight index funds, international stocks, small-cap funds, or bonds to offset the mega-cap tilt. Others simply understand the risk and stay the course. Both are valid approaches.

The point isn't to scare you out of your index fund. It's to make sure you know what you actually own.

## About This Analysis

All data comes from the Financial Modeling Prep (FMP) API, covering ~498 S&P 500 constituents with 5 years of daily stock prices (Jan 2021 - Feb 2026) and 20 quarters of financial statements. Returns are price-only (no dividend reinvestment), which means actual total returns would be slightly higher — particularly for dividend-heavy sectors like Energy and Utilities. The analysis uses today's S&P 500 members only, which means companies that were removed from the index during this period (because they were acquired, went bankrupt, or simply shrank) aren't included. This "survivorship bias" means the "other 490" group looks slightly better than it would if we included the companies that fell out along the way. Market cap estimates use approximations based on current values and cumulative returns. The full dataset, methodology notes, and Python analysis code are available in the project repository.
