# The Trillion-Dollar Shell Game: Where Your 401(k) Money Actually Goes

*A plain-English breakdown of what S&P 500 companies are doing with their cash — and why it matters for your retirement account. This is for informational and educational purposes only, not financial advice.*

---

Every quarter, the 500 largest public companies in America make a decision that directly affects your retirement savings, even if you never hear about it. They decide what to do with their profits. They can invest in new factories, hire more people, develop new products — or they can send cash back to the people who own the stock.

Over the past five years, those companies earned roughly $10 trillion in profit. What they did with it tells the real story of modern American capitalism, and if you have a 401(k) or an index fund, it's your money they're playing with.

I analyzed five years of financial statements for every company in the S&P 500. Here's what I found.

## The Trillion-Dollar Buyback Machine

S&P 500 companies spent roughly **$1 trillion per year** buying back their own stock in each of the last four years. In 2025 alone, they spent $979 billion on buybacks and paid out $641 billion in dividends. That's **$1.6 trillion** returned to shareholders in a single year.

To put that in perspective, the entire federal education budget is about $80 billion. These 500 companies sent twenty times that amount back to their shareholders.

But what does "buying back stock" actually mean for you?

### Stock Buybacks, Explained Like You're Not on Wall Street

When a company buys back its own stock, it's using its cash to purchase shares on the open market and retire them — essentially making them disappear. Fewer shares outstanding means each remaining share represents a bigger piece of the company.

Think of it like a pizza. If you and nine friends own a pizza cut into 10 slices and three friends sell their slices back to the pizza shop, the shop throws those slices away. Now the same pizza is split 7 ways instead of 10. Your slice didn't get bigger, but your *share* of the whole pizza did.

For your index fund, buybacks work in the background. They prop up earnings-per-share (EPS) — the number Wall Street cares most about — even when total profits are flat. If a company earns the same $10 billion as last year but has 5% fewer shares, its EPS magically goes up 5%. The stock price follows.

This is why Apple, the biggest buyback spender at **$66 billion per year**, has seen its stock more than double in five years despite revenue growth that's been relatively modest. Apple isn't just selling more iPhones — it's steadily shrinking its share count, concentrating its profits into fewer and fewer slices.

### The Top 20 Rule

Here's a pattern that's held for years: roughly **half of all buyback spending comes from just 20 companies**. In the most recent 12 months, the top 20 spenders accounted for $346 billion out of $693 billion total — almost exactly 50%.

The biggest spenders:

| Company | Annual Buyback Spending |
|---------|----------------------|
| Apple | $65.9 billion |
| NVIDIA | $36.3 billion |
| Alphabet (Google) | $30.6 billion |
| Microsoft | $17.6 billion |
| JPMorgan Chase | $15.8 billion |

Apple's buyback program is so massive that it spends more repurchasing its own stock each year than most S&P 500 companies are *worth*.

## The Hidden Tax on Your Returns (That Nobody Talks About)

Here's the part of the buyback story that gets much less attention: a significant chunk of those buybacks are being canceled out before they ever benefit you.

It's called stock-based compensation, or SBC. When tech companies pay their employees partly in stock — which most of them do — they're creating new shares. Those new shares dilute existing owners, including you and your index fund.

In 2025, S&P 500 companies spent **$979 billion on buybacks** but simultaneously issued **$232 billion in stock-based compensation** to employees. That's like filling a bathtub while the drain is open — only about 75 cents of every buyback dollar actually reduces the share count.

And it's much worse in certain sectors. Technology companies spent $404 billion on buybacks in 2025 but gave away $153 billion in stock comp — a **38% offset**. For every $3 they spent buying stock back, they handed $1 worth of new stock to employees.

| Sector | Buybacks | Stock Comp | Net Effect | % Cancelled Out |
|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Technology | $404B | $153B | $252B | 38% |
| Financial Services | $192B | $16B | $176B | 8% |
| Healthcare | $82B | $14B | $68B | 17% |
| Energy | $57B | $1B | $56B | 2% |

Financial Services and Energy companies are the most efficient — nearly every buyback dollar counts. Tech companies are the least efficient. When you see a headline saying "Tech companies are spending hundreds of billions on buybacks," know that more than a third of that is being offset by dilution.

## Is Your Slice Actually Getting Bigger?

Despite the dilution issue, buybacks *are* meaningfully shrinking share counts for many companies. In 2025, **72% of S&P 500 companies** reduced their share count year-over-year, and the median company shrank its float by 1.33%.

There's a benchmark that market analysts use called the **"4% Rule"** — if a company reduces its share count by at least 4% in a year, it's making a material difference in earnings per share. In 2025, 66 companies hit that mark, up from 44 the year before.

The most aggressive share reducers weren't the names you'd expect:

| Company | Shares Reduced | How They Did It |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Southwest Airlines | 18.7% | Retired nearly 1 in 5 shares |
| General Motors | 18.6% | Massive buyback program |
| PVH Corp. (Calvin Klein) | 14.9% | Aggressive repurchases |
| AIG | 12.9% | Post-restructuring buybacks |
| Charter Communications | 11.3% | Debt-funded repurchases |

Southwest Airlines retired nearly a fifth of its outstanding shares in a single year. Whether that's a smart use of cash for an airline or financial engineering that papers over mediocre operations is a question worth asking. But for shareholders, the math is clear: your slice of Southwest got meaningfully bigger.

## Dividends: The Quiet Shift

While buybacks get the headlines, dividends have a longer history and more emotional attachment for many investors — especially retirees who depend on them for income. Here's what's been happening.

**Dividend growth has stalled.** After strong increases in 2022 (+11%) and 2023 (+12%), total dividend payouts actually *declined* in 2024 and 2025. The S&P 500's dividend yield sits at just **0.73%** — well below the historical average of roughly 2%.

This isn't because companies are struggling. It's a choice. Companies are *choosing* to send more cash through buybacks and less through dividends. Over the trailing 12 months, S&P 500 companies returned $693 billion via buybacks versus $467 billion in dividends — roughly a 60/40 split favoring buybacks.

Why? Partly because buybacks are more tax-efficient (you don't owe taxes on a buyback until you sell), partly because they give management more flexibility (you can pause a buyback quietly; cutting a dividend makes headlines), and partly because Wall Street rewards EPS growth, which buybacks directly boost.

**The good news for dividend investors:** 79% of companies that pay dividends *raised* them in 2025. Only 20 companies cut their dividends — a 5% rate. The per-share commitment to dividends is still growing, even as total dollar payouts have dipped.

Some notable cuts tell their own stories:

- **Celanese** slashed its dividend 96% amid a debt crisis from acquisitions
- **Estee Lauder** cut 40% as its business fundamentals deteriorated
- **Diamondback Energy** and **Devon Energy** cut 50% and 34% respectively, but that's how energy dividends work — they rise and fall with oil prices by design

Meanwhile, 248 companies — two-thirds of all dividend payers — raised their dividend every single year for the past four years straight. Industrials and Financial Services lead that pack, with boring-but-reliable names you'd recognize: banks, utilities, manufacturers.

## The Market's Vital Signs: Mostly Healthy, With Asterisks

### The Headline Numbers

The S&P 500 has returned **77% over the past five years** — about 12% annually. That's right in line with the long-term historical average. If you stayed invested through the 2022 bear market (a 25% drawdown that took about two years to fully recover), you've been well rewarded.

| Year | S&P 500 Return |
|------|---------------|
| 2021 | +21% |
| 2022 | -20% |
| 2023 | +25% |
| 2024 | +24% |
| 2025 | +17% |

Two consecutive years of ~24% gains in 2023-2024 were exceptional. The market moderated in 2025 and has started 2026 on a mild positive note.

### Most Companies Are Profitable (But Margins Are Under Pressure)

In the most recent quarter, **94% of S&P 500 companies reported positive earnings**. That's a strong profitability rate.

But there's a more cautious signal beneath the surface. While revenue is growing broadly — three out of four companies grew their top line in most recent quarters — *earnings* breadth has been much thinner. In the second half of 2025, the number of companies with improving profits was barely more than the number with declining profits.

Translation: companies are selling more, but they're not all keeping more. Costs are rising, margins are compressing, and the gap between "growing revenue" and "growing profits" is the early warning sign worth watching.

### Earnings Quality Is Actually Improving

There's a metric that the legendary S&P analyst Howard Silverblatt tracks obsessively: the gap between "operating earnings" (what companies say they earned) and "GAAP earnings" (what the accounting rules say they earned). The bigger the gap, the more companies are using one-time items, write-offs, and adjustments to make their numbers look better than reality.

In 2022, that gap was 28% — meaning companies' self-reported earnings were 28% higher than their official accounting numbers. By the second half of 2025, the gap had compressed to about 20%, the tightest since 2021.

This is genuinely good news. It means the earnings being reported are closer to reality than they've been in years. Technology companies have the cleanest books — a 12.5% gap. Basic Materials has the messiest — 44%. If you're evaluating a sector, this is a data point worth knowing.

## What the Price Tag Says

The S&P 500 currently trades at a P/E ratio of **37.9x** on a GAAP basis (about 30x on operating earnings). The historical average is around 20-22x.

In plain English: investors are paying roughly $38 for every $1 of earnings, compared to a typical $20-22. Either these companies will grow into that valuation — earnings will rise fast enough to justify the price — or at some point, prices will adjust.

This doesn't mean a crash is imminent. Markets have traded at elevated valuations for years at a time. But it does mean the math for future returns gets harder. When you buy at 38x earnings, you need more growth to justify your investment than when you buy at 20x.

The total market cap of the S&P 500 is now **$63.9 trillion**. Against trailing earnings of $1.7 trillion (GAAP) and $2.1 trillion (operating), the market is pricing in significant earnings growth over the coming years.

## Technology: The Sector That Runs Everything

Technology is now **46% of the S&P 500 by market cap** — nearly as much as the bottom eight sectors combined. It leads the index on virtually every dimension:

- **Highest profit margins**: 30.5% operating margin (the next closest sector is 25%)
- **Cleanest earnings**: smallest gap between reported and actual profits
- **Biggest buyback spender**: $404 billion in 2025
- **Biggest dividend payer**: $99 billion in trailing dividends (yes, more than traditionally "dividend" sectors like Utilities)
- **Biggest employer stock comp**: $153 billion — where the dilution problem lives

If you own an S&P 500 index fund, you own more Technology than anything else by a wide margin. Whether that concentration is a feature or a bug depends on whether you believe these companies' growth will continue justifying their dominance. So far, the earnings have backed it up — Tech's 30% margins dwarf every other sector. But it's worth knowing that nearly half your "diversified" index fund is a bet on one sector.

## What This Means for Your Retirement Account

If you're a passive investor — and again, I think that's the right approach for most people — here are the things worth understanding:

**Your companies are returning $1.6 trillion a year to shareholders.** Between buybacks and dividends, S&P 500 companies are sending an enormous amount of cash back to their owners. This is a net positive for long-term stock returns, even if the mechanism (buybacks vs. dividends) is shifting.

**Buybacks are now the primary channel.** If you're a younger investor who doesn't need current income, this shift toward buybacks over dividends is actually in your favor — it's more tax-efficient and drives stock price appreciation, which compounds in your retirement account. If you're retired and living on dividends, the shrinking yield (0.73%) means you might need to supplement with capital gains or look at higher-yield alternatives.

**Watch the dilution.** Not all buybacks are created equal. A $10 billion buyback sounds impressive, but if the company simultaneously issued $4 billion in stock to employees, the net effect is only $6 billion. Financial Services and Energy companies give you the most bang for your buyback buck. Tech companies, despite spending the most, lose over a third to dilution.

**Valuations are elevated.** A 38x P/E doesn't mean stocks will fall tomorrow. But it does mean the market is priced for continued strong growth. If earnings disappoint — whether from a recession, trade disruption, or AI investments not paying off as expected — there's less room for error at these levels.

**The earnings are real, but the margins are under pressure.** Revenue is growing broadly, but an increasing number of companies are struggling to translate that growth into higher profits. This is worth watching. If margin compression spreads to the mega-caps, the whole index will feel it.

The stock market is ultimately a machine that turns corporate profits into returns for regular people's retirement accounts. Right now, that machine is running well — $10 trillion in profits over five years, $8 trillion returned to shareholders, 77% cumulative returns. But the inner workings are more concentrated, more dependent on buybacks, and more richly valued than at any point in recent memory.

Understanding those mechanics won't change your investment strategy. But it should change how you think about that line item in your 401(k) statement.
