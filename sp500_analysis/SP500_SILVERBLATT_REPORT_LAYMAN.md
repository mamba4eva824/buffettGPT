# The S&P 500: WTF Is Going On With the Stock Market?
### A Plain English Breakdown | Data as of February 11, 2026

**Who is this for?** You. The person with a Robinhood account, some money in a 401(k) you barely understand, and a vague feeling that you should "be investing." This report covers what America's 500 biggest companies are actually doing with their money — and what it means for yours.

**What's the S&P 500?** Think of it as the varsity team of American business. It's a list of the ~500 largest publicly traded companies in the US — Apple, Amazon, JPMorgan, your health insurance company, the people who make your cereal. When people say "the market," they usually mean this.

**How do you buy it?** The easiest way is through SPY, an ETF (basically a bundle of all 500 stocks in one purchase). When we say "SPY returned 16.6%," that means if you'd put $1,000 into SPY at the start of 2025, you'd have roughly $1,166 at year-end.

## 3. Buybacks: The Thing Nobody Explains to You

### What's a Buyback?

Imagine a pizza cut into 100 slices, and you own 1 slice (1%). A buyback is when the company buys 10 slices and throws them away. Now there are only 90 slices, and your 1 slice is worth 1/90th instead of 1/100th of the pizza. **Your slice just got bigger without you doing anything.**

Companies spent roughly **$1 TRILLION per year** buying back their own stock in 2022-2025. That's not a typo. A trillion dollars, every single year.

| Year | Spent on Buybacks | Why It Matters |
|------|------------------|---------------|
| 2021 | $946 Billion | Post-COVID spending spree begins |
| 2022 | $1,055 Billion | Peak buyback year |
| 2023 | $979 Billion | Consistent |
| 2024 | $990 Billion | Consistent |
| 2025 | $979 Billion | Consistent |

### But Here's the Catch: Stock-Based Compensation (The "Employee Tax")

Companies are buying back stock with one hand and giving it away to employees with the other. When a company pays its engineers and executives in stock instead of cash, it creates NEW shares — which makes YOUR slice smaller again.

| Year | Bought Back (shrinks share count) | Gave to Employees (grows share count) | Net Effect |
|------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|-----------|
| 2021 | $946B | $176B (19% offset) | $770B actually helped you |
| 2022 | $1,055B | $219B (21% offset) | $836B actually helped you |
| 2023 | $979B | $256B (26% offset) | $723B actually helped you |
| 2024 | $990B | $230B (23% offset) | $760B actually helped you |
| 2025 | $979B | $232B (24% offset) | $747B actually helped you |

**Translation:** About 1 in 4 buyback dollars gets canceled out by stock-based compensation. It's like filling a bathtub while the drain is partially open.

Apple alone spent $66 billion buying back its own stock in 12 months. That's more than the next two companies combined. If you own Apple shares, the company is actively trying to make each of your shares more valuable by shrinking the total pool.

### The "Bathtub Problem" by Sector

Some sectors are much worse at the buyback game because they give away so much stock to employees:

| Sector | Bought Back | Gave to Employees | How Much Was Wasted | Grade |
|--------|------------|-------------------|-------------------|----|

**The big insight:** Tech companies spend the most on buybacks ($404B) but also give away the most in employee stock ($153B). For every $3 they spend buying back stock, they give away more than $1 in new stock. Banks and energy companies are way more efficient — almost every dollar they spend on buybacks actually shrinks the share count.

### Companies Shrinking Their Slice Count the Fastest

Some companies are so aggressive about buybacks that they're deleting huge chunks of their own stock. The "4% Rule" says if a company reduces its share count by 4%+ per year, it meaningfully boosts your earnings per share.

**Why this matters to you:** When Southwest deletes 18.7% of its shares, your 1 share of LUV now represents a bigger piece of the company. If nothing else changes, your earnings per share just went up ~23% purely from math. That's why companies do this — it's a way to "manufacture" higher earnings per share without actually earning more money.

## 4. Are Companies Being Honest About Their Profits?

### The Two Versions of Profit

Every company reports two profit numbers:
- **Operating earnings** ("adjusted") — what the company *says* it earned, after removing "one-time" charges. This is the flattering selfie.
- **GAAP earnings** ("real") — what the company actually earned by the official accounting rules. This is the no-filter photo.

The gap between them tells you how honest a company is being.

| Year | Total Sales | "Adjusted" Profit | Real Profit | The Gap |
|------|-----------|------------------|------------|---------|
| 2021 | $15.4 Trillion | $2.55T | $2.06T | 19% gap |
| 2022 | $17.5T | $2.46T | $1.77T | 28% gap (they were hiding a lot) |
| 2023 | $18.6T | $2.67T | $2.10T | 21% gap |
| 2024 | $17.7T | $2.65T | $1.98T | 25% gap |
| 2025 | $18.3T | $2.80T | $2.20T | **21.5% gap** (getting more honest) |

**What the gap means:** In 2025, for every $1.00 companies said they earned, only about $0.79 actually showed up in real (GAAP) profit. The other 21 cents disappeared in restructuring charges, write-downs, and other stuff they'd rather you not focus on.

Good news: this gap is shrinking. In 2022, companies were hiding 28% of the difference. Now it's down to 21.5%. More honest = better for investors.

### How Much Profit Does Each Sector Actually Keep?

| Sector | What They Sell | Profit After Running Costs | How Honest Are They? |
|--------|--------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Technology | $3.2 Trillion | Keeps 30.5 cents per dollar | Very honest (12.5% gap) |
| Financial Services | $2.3T | Keeps 21.6 cents | Mostly honest (24% gap) |
| Utilities | $0.4T | Keeps 22.7 cents | Somewhat sketchy (38% gap) |
| Healthcare | $3.5T | Keeps 7.7 cents | Meh (33% gap) |
| Basic Materials | $0.4T | Keeps 11.2 cents | Sketchiest (44% gap!) |

**Translation:** Technology makes the most profit AND is the most honest about it. Basic Materials companies have the biggest gap between what they *say* they earn and what they *actually* earn — nearly half the "profit" disappears when you use real accounting rules.

### What's a Dividend?

When you own a share of stock, some companies send you cash just for being an owner — usually every 3 months. It's like getting a rent check from your investment. Not all companies do this, but about 80% of S&P 500 companies do.

**What happened?** After two strong growth years (2022-2023), total dividend payments actually *declined* in 2024 and 2025. Companies are keeping more of their profits to fund buybacks and invest in AI, expansion, and other growth plays instead of sending cash to shareholders.

### The Dividend Yield Reality Check

If you buy the S&P 500 today, you'd earn a **0.73% dividend yield**. Meaning for every $10,000 invested, you'd get about **$73 per year** in dividend payments.

For context, the historical average is around 2% ($200 per year on $10,000). Why is it so low now? Because stock prices have gone up so much that the dividend payments haven't kept pace. The companies are still paying — it just looks small relative to today's sky-high stock prices.

## 6. The Price Tag: Is the Market Expensive?

### What You're Paying

| The Price Tag | What It Means | Today's Number | Is That Normal? |
|--------------|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| Total market value | What all 500 companies are worth combined | **$63.9 Trillion** | That's bigger than the GDP of most continents |

| P/E Ratio (adjusted profits) | Same thing but using the flattering version | **29.8x** | Still above average |

**In plain English:** If you buy the S&P 500 today, you're paying about 30-38 years' worth of profits. Historically, you'd pay 20-22 years' worth. The market is expensive by historical standards — not insanely so, but definitely "paying full price."

**What this means for your returns:** When you buy stocks at high valuations, your future returns tend to be lower. Not negative necessarily — just lower than the 12% annual average we've enjoyed. Think 6-8% annually over the next decade instead of 12%. That's still better than a savings account, but manage your expectations.

## Key Takeaways for Real People

### 1. Your $100/month is working. Keep going.
The S&P 500 returned 12% annually over the past 5 years — right in line with its 100-year average. If you're auto-investing into an index fund every month, the math is on your side over the long run.

### 2. Tech is almost half your index fund.
When you buy SPY or any S&P 500 fund, 46% of your money goes to tech companies. That's great when tech is booming, scary when it's not. Just know what you own.

### 3. Buybacks matter more than dividends now.
Companies spent $979B on buybacks vs $641B on dividends in 2025. Buybacks are a less visible way to return value to you — instead of sending you a check, they make each share you own a slightly bigger piece of the pie. But watch out for the stock-based compensation offset — especially in tech.

### 4. The "are profits real?" question matters.
There's a 21.5% gap between what companies SAY they earn and what they actually earn by the rules. That gap is shrinking (good sign), but always be a little skeptical when companies tout their "adjusted" earnings.

### 5. Dividends aren't dead — they're just less flashy.
79% of companies raised their dividend in 2025. The yield looks tiny (0.73%) because stock prices are high, not because companies stopped paying. If you're building a long-term portfolio, dividend growers are still powerful compounders.

### 6. The market is priced for perfection.
At 30-38x earnings, the market needs things to keep going well to justify today's prices. That doesn't mean a crash is coming — it means your future returns from this price point will likely be more modest than the last 5 years. Plan accordingly: invest consistently, keep an emergency fund, and don't bet money you'll need in 2-3 years.
