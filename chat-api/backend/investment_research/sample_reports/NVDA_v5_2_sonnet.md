## TL;DR

## What Does NVDA Actually Do?

NVIDIA designs the semiconductors — specifically GPUs (graphics processing units) — that power artificial intelligence workloads. Their H100 and upcoming Blackwell chips are essentially the pick-and-shovel of the AI gold rush: every company racing to build AI products needs NVDA's chips to do it. They sell directly to hyperscalers like Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon, as well as to enterprise data centers and AI startups. Revenue is primarily from chip sales, but NVIDIA also has a software layer (CUDA ecosystem) that creates deep switching costs. The last time you used ChatGPT, Gemini, or practically any modern AI product, you were almost certainly using compute that ran on NVDA hardware.

## Profitability: 53% Net Margins — The Most Profitable Chip Company on Earth

NVDA's margins are not normal. Most tech companies celebrate hitting 20% net margins. NVDA is sitting at 53% — meaning for every dollar of revenue that comes in the door, they keep 53 cents after paying everyone, building future chips, running the company, and paying taxes. For a hardware company that actually manufactures (through TSMC) physical chips, this is almost absurd. To understand how rare this is: Apple's net margin is around 25%. That's NVDA's floor, not their ceiling.

**Where Does Each Dollar Go? (The NVDA Money Waterfall)**

This is where the NVDA story gets genuinely surprising. Unlike most hardware companies where cost of goods eats 50-60 cents of every dollar, NVDA manages to keep cost of goods down to about 30 cents because their chips command such extraordinary pricing power.

| Step | What Happens | Amount (TTM) | What's Left |
|------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Revenue** | Total chip sales come in | $187.1B | 100% |
| **Cost of Chips** | Making chips via TSMC | ~$55.9B | ~70% left |
| = **Gross Profit** | After manufacturing costs | ~$131.2B | **70.1%** |
| **R&D** | Building next-gen GPUs | ~$11.2B | — |
| **Sales & Marketing** | Selling to hyperscalers | ~$4.7B | — |
| **Admin/Overhead** | Running the company | ~$3.7B | — |
| = **Operating Profit** | After all operating costs | ~$111.6B | **~59.6%** |
| **Interest & Taxes** | Loan payments + government | ~$12.4B | — |
| = **Net Profit** | What hits the bottom line | ~$99.2B | **53.0%** |

What's genuinely surprising here: NVDA's cost of goods is only about 30 cents per dollar of revenue. For a company selling physical chips, that pricing power — charging $30,000–$40,000+ per H100 GPU — is the whole story. The chip's raw material cost is a fraction of what NVDA charges because there's simply no alternative for serious AI workloads.

**5-Year Margin Journey:**

| Year | Revenue | Gross Margin | Operating Margin | Net Margin | Margin Meter |
|------|---------|-------------|-----------------|------------|--------------|
| 2022 | $26.9B | 64.9% | 37.3% | 36.2% | 🟡 Pre-AI baseline |
| 2023 | $27.0B | 56.9% | 16.3% | 16.2% | 🔴 Gaming downturn |
| 2024 | $60.9B | 72.7% | 54.1% | 48.8% | 🚀 AI ramp inflection |
| 2025 | $130.5B | 75.0% | 61.9% | 55.8% | 🚀 Peak efficiency |
| **TTM** | **$187.1B** | **70.1%** | **~59.6%** | **53.0%** | 🟢 Slight gross margin dip from product mix |

**Why Margins Are Changing (Operating Leverage):**
NVDA's current slight gross margin compression — from 75% in FY2025 to 70.1% TTM — is driven by the Blackwell chip transition. New chip architectures launch with higher initial production costs at TSMC as yields ramp up, which temporarily compresses gross margins before improving as manufacturing matures. This is standard semiconductor cycle behavior: Hopper (H100) margins were lower when it first launched too. R&D spending has stayed relatively flat as a percentage of revenue, while revenue has grown dramatically, so the operating leverage story remains intact. NVDA's ability to grow revenue faster than its cost structure is why operating margins expanded from 16% in 2023 to nearly 60% in 2025.

**Peer Context:**
NVDA's 53% net margin is dramatically above the semiconductor industry average of 15–20% and well above even elite peers — AMD runs around 6–8% net margin, Intel is currently negative. The closest comparison in the broader tech world is Microsoft (~35%) and Apple (~25%). NVDA has essentially priced their chips like software.

**Efficiency Grade (ROE):**
With a TTM ROE of 104.4%, NVDA earns an A+ on efficiency — that means the company generates $1.04 of annual profit for every $1 shareholders have invested. It's worth noting that while ROE above 100% sounds almost too good to be true, NVDA achieves this primarily through extraordinary profit margins rather than leverage — their debt-to-equity is only 0.09x, so this isn't a debt-inflated metric.

**Bottom line:** + NVDA's 53% net margins and 104% ROE reflect genuine pricing power and platform dominance — this is what software-like margins look like in hardware.

## Earnings Quality: The $8.6B Adjustment — Small Gap, Clean Books

Some companies look profitable on paper, but when you actually check the bank account, the cash has mysteriously evaporated. NVDA isn't one of those companies — the gap between what they report and what actually hits the bank is relatively small and easy to explain.

| **Total** | **"Adjusted" Earnings** | | **$107.8B** |

The gap between GAAP and adjusted is about $8.6B, or roughly 8% — that's in the "clean books" territory.

**What's Getting Added Back?**

| What They Removed | Amount | Is This Legit? | Plain English |
|-------------------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Depreciation & Amortization | $2.6B | ✅ Usually yes | Wear and tear on NVDA's server equipment and buildings — real cost but no cash leaves the building this quarter |
| Stock-Based Compensation | $6.1B | ❌ Debatable | NVDA pays their world-class AI engineers with stock — this IS a real cost, it just dilutes your ownership rather than spending cash |

**Important timing note:** The $2.6B in D&A represents the gradual spreading of real cash NVDA already spent on data centers, servers, and equipment. Think of it like NVDA bought a data center for $10B — they don't expense all $10B this year, they expense a fraction each year. When they "add back" D&A, they're not hiding costs; they're showing profit before that spreading occurs.

Over the last three years, NVDA's cash-to-profit ratio has been 0.84x (TTM) — meaning for every $1 of reported profit, $0.84 actually showed up in the bank. The slight compression from 0.94x to 0.84x reflects faster growth requiring more working capital (building inventory for Blackwell), not a quality concern.

## Bear Case

**1. Growth Deceleration Accelerates — The "Law of Large Numbers" Hits Hard**
NVDA grew from $27B to $187B in three years. Growing at even 20% from here requires finding $37B in new revenue annually — larger than the entire annual revenue of most semiconductor companies. This isn't just theoretical: every percentage point of growth rate compression at this scale changes the valuation math dramatically. If FY2027 revenue comes in at $280B instead of $353B, that's a 20% miss that could compress multiples and share price.

AI infrastructure buildouts are notoriously cyclical. The hyperscalers are spending $100B+ combined in 2025 — if ROI on AI investments disappoints (no breakthrough consumer AI application, models not yet generating enough revenue), CapEx cuts could come swiftly. NVDA has limited revenue diversification away from data center AI. One bad CapEx season could mean a 20-30% revenue miss.

**3. Export Controls and China Risk Compounds**
US export controls already cost NVDA significant China revenue. If controls tighten further or expand to additional geographies, NVDA loses access to markets that would otherwise be strong AI adopters. The geopolitical situation is unpredictable, and each new restriction adds to the revenue TAM that NVDA can't address. This isn't just theoretical — it's already happened.

**4. AMD + Custom Silicon Close the Performance Gap Faster Than Expected**
ROCm software improving, AMD's MI300X gaining traction for certain inference workloads, and hyperscaler custom chips (Google TPUs, Amazon Trainium) getting more capable. If NVDA's performance lead narrows from "dramatically better" to "marginally better," pricing power erodes. Even a 10-15% reduction in average selling prices at this revenue scale would be a multi-billion dollar hit to gross margin.

**The Nightmare Scenario:** If hyperscaler CapEx pulls back 20-30% due to AI ROI disappointment AND AMD gains meaningful share in the inference market AND export controls expand, NVDA could see FY2027 revenue closer to $200-250B vs. consensus $353B. In that scenario — where growth normalizes toward 15-20% instead of 65% — multiple compression from 36x P/E toward 20-25x could represent significant valuation risk.
