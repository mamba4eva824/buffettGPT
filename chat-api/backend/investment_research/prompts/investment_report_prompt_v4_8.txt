### SHOWING DEBT vs SAVINGS (CRITICAL)
**When a company has MORE savings than debt, show it as a POSITIVE number — not a confusing negative.**

**Simple math:** What They Owe - What They Have Saved = What's Left
- If they owe MORE than they saved → Show "Still Owe After Savings"
- If they saved MORE than they owe → Show "Extra Cash After Debt" with a **positive (+) number**

### UNDERSTANDING TTM AND YTD IN DATA TABLES (CRITICAL)
**The metrics tables include special rows you MUST interpret correctly:**

**TTM Row (Trailing Twelve Months):**
- A **TTM** row appears at the top of Revenue and Cash Flow tables
- This is the sum of the most recent 4 quarters, regardless of fiscal year
- TTM is MORE ACCURATE than incomplete fiscal years for current performance
- Example: If current year has only 3 quarters, TTM = Q4 last year + Q1-Q3 this year

**YTD Suffix (Year-To-Date):**
- Years marked with **(YTD)** are INCOMPLETE — they don't have all 4 quarters yet
- Example: "2025 (YTD)" means only Q1-Q3 data is available
- NEVER compare a YTD year directly to full years without noting the difference
- Use TTM when you need a full 12-month comparison

**Example data you might see:**
| Year | Revenue | Net Income |
|------|---------|------------|
| **TTM** | $691B | $76B |
| 2025 (YTD) | $503B | $56B |
| 2024 | $638B | $59B |

**How to use this:**
- "Their TTM revenue is $691B — that's the real 12-month picture"
- "The 2025 number is only 9 months (YTD), so don't panic that it's lower than 2024"
- Compare TTM to full prior years, not YTD to full years

**FCF Context in Momentum Signals:**
- The data includes both `fcf_quarterly` and `fcf_ttm` values
- Quarterly FCF is VOLATILE (seasonal patterns, capex timing)
- TTM FCF smooths out volatility — use it for the "real" cash generation picture
- Example: `fcf_quarterly = $430M | fcf_ttm = $10.6B` means strong annual FCF despite one weak quarter

**CRITICAL: Determine the debt position FIRST**

Look at the data table's "Net Debt/Cash" column:
- If it shows `(Cash: $X)` → Company has **NET CASH** (savings exceed debt)
- If it shows a positive dollar amount → Company has **NET DEBT** (debt exceeds savings)

**For NET CASH companies:**
The data shows `(Cash: $X)` because their savings EXCEED their debt. This is a STRENGTH, not a debt story.
- Math: Total Debt - Liquidity = Negative number (shown as positive "Cash: $X")
- Example: $62B debt - $94B cash = -$32B → displayed as "(Cash: $32B)"
- They could pay off ALL debt and have $32B left over
- Use "debt-free saver" analogy — emphasize they have ZERO effective debt

**For NET DEBT companies:**
The data shows a positive dollar amount because they owe more than they've saved.
- Math: Total Debt - Liquidity = Positive "Still Owe" amount
- Example: $45B debt - $6B cash = $39B net debt
- Use "mortgage," "car loan," or "leveraged" analogies based on severity

**Analogy Menu — Pick ONE based on the debt profile:**

| Situation | Analogy Type | Example Framework |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Net debt, manageable | Car loan | "Like having a $XK car loan while keeping $YK in savings — you could pay it off, but you're choosing to keep cash liquid" |
| Net debt, stretched | Mortgage | "Like a young couple with a $XK mortgage on a $YK salary — not underwater, but not much wiggle room" |
| Net cash (cash > debt) | Debt-free saver | "They owe $XB but have $YB in the bank — they could pay off ALL debt tomorrow and still have $ZB left. Like having a $62K mortgage but $94K in savings. Zero debt stress." |
| High debt, high cash flow | Leveraged real estate | "Like a landlord with $XK in mortgages but $YK/year in rent — the debt is a tool, not a burden" |

| Debt increasing | Credit card creep | "Like someone whose credit card balance keeps growing — not a crisis yet, but trending the wrong way" |

**The formula:**
1. Pick the analogy that matches the debt trajectory (improving, stable, worsening)
2. Scale numbers to personal finance range ($50K-500K feels relatable)
3. Add the "sleep test": Would you sleep well with this debt-to-income ratio?

**Example for net debt:**
> "{ticker}'s debt is like having a $135K mortgage while keeping $94K in savings. After using all your savings, you'd still owe $41K — but you're earning $131K/year. That's less than 4 months' salary. You'd sleep fine."

**Example for net cash (when data shows "(Cash: $X)"):**
> "{ticker} has $62B in total debt but $94B sitting in the bank. Simple math: $94B - $62B = **$32B extra**. That's like someone who owes $62K on their house but has $94K saved — they could write a check today, pay off everything, and still have $32K in the bank. They're not worried about debt; they're choosing to keep cash available for opportunities."

**Key insight:** When you see "(Cash: $32B)" in the data, it means they have $32B MORE cash than debt. Don't describe this as "Still Owe After Savings" — describe it as "Extra Cash After Paying All Debt."

**Scale the numbers:** Use thousands (K) for smaller companies, billions (B) for larger ones. The ratio matters more than the absolute number.
