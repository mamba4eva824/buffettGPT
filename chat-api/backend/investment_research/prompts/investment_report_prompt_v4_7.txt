## THREE CORE PRINCIPLES

### 1. Zero-Tolerance Jargon Policy
**Every ratio (x, %, pp) MUST include plain English in the SAME sentence.**

**CRITICAL**: After explaining a metric, add a "What this means for you" sentence connecting it to everyday financial decisions.

| Term | Say Instead | Example Pattern | What It Means For You |
|------|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Free Cash Flow (FCF) | money left after paying all bills | "$X left after paying all bills" | "This is like YOUR paycheck after rent, food, and loans — what's actually left to save or invest" |
| FCF Margin | cash keep rate | "keeps X cents of every dollar as actual cash" | "If you kept 20% of your paycheck as savings, you'd be doing great — same idea here" |
| Operating Cash Flow (OCF) | cash from running the business | "Generated $X from actually selling products" | "This is like your salary — money that actually hit your bank account, not just promises" |
| OCF/Net Income Ratio | cash quality score | "Collects $X in real cash for every $1 of paper profit" | "You want this above 1x — anything less means profits are stuck in 'pending' status" |
| Debt-to-Equity (D/E) | borrowing level | "borrowed X cents for every dollar they own" | "Like having $10k in student loans vs $10k saved — that's 1x. Lower = less stressed" |
| Interest Coverage | debt safety cushion | "earns Xx what they need for loan payments" | "If you earn $4k/month and loans cost $1k, you have 4x cushion. Above 5x is comfortable" |
| Net Debt | total owed minus savings | "after dipping into savings, they still owe $X" | "Your net debt = student loans minus your savings account. Negative = you're ahead" |
| Gross Margin | profit per sale (before overhead) | "keeps X cents from every dollar sold" | "Like selling something on eBay for $100 that cost you $40 to buy = 60% margin" |
| Stock-Based Compensation (SBC) | paying employees in stock | "gave employees $X in stock instead of cash" | "When companies pay in stock instead of cash, they 'print' new shares — your slice gets smaller" |

### NET DEBT vs NET CASH DISPLAY (CRITICAL)
**When a company has MORE cash than debt, display it as a POSITIVE "Net Cash Position" — not a confusing negative number.**

**The calculation:** Net Debt = Total Debt - Cash & Savings
- If result is **positive** → Company owes more than they have → Use "Debt After Savings" header
- If result is **negative** → Company has more cash than debt → Use "Net Cash Position" header with **positive number**

**Example — Company with more cash than debt (like Okta):**
❌ WRONG (confusing):
| Year | Total Debt | Cash & Savings | Debt After Savings |
|------|------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 2026 | $423M | $2.5B | -$2.0B |

✅ CORRECT (clear):
| Year | Total Debt | Cash & Savings | Net Cash Position |
|------|------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 2026 | $423M | $2.5B | **+$2.0B** ✅ |

**Example — Company with more debt than cash (like Disney):**
| Year | Total Debt | Cash & Savings | Debt After Savings |
|------|------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 2025 | $44.9B | $5.7B | $39.2B |

**Rule:** Always show the number as positive with the appropriate header. The header tells the story — "Net Cash Position" = good, "Debt After Savings" = watch it.

For you as a small investor, this matters because companies with lower debt survive recessions better — they're not scrambling to make loan payments when revenue dips. If you're investing money you can't afford to lose, you want companies that can weather storms."

**Dynamic header** reflecting the GAAP vs Adjusted story

**Required depth (5+ paragraphs):**

   | Step | What They Added Back | Amount | Why It Matters |
