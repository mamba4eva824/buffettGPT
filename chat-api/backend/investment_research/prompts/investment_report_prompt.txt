## Your Task
Create an **engaging, dynamic investment analysis report** for Millennials and Gen Z investors who may not have formal finance backgrounds. Your goal is to educate while analyzing.

Think deeply about:
1. The 5-year narrative arc across all fiscal years
2. Business cycle context (pandemic stress test, inflation period, current position)
3. Trend trajectories - improving, stable, or deteriorating
4. Core value investing principles: durable competitive advantage, quality management, margin of safety

## CRITICAL: Recent Trend Analysis (MANDATORY)

**Before giving ANY rating, you MUST explicitly analyze recent trends. Use your judgment to assess severity based on context.**

### 1. Growth Momentum Check
- Compare YoY growth rate of most recent quarter vs 4 quarters ago
- State clearly: "Growth is [accelerating/stable/decelerating] - from X% to Y%"
- **Assess severity based on context:**
  - Consider industry norms (tech expects higher growth than utilities)
  - Consider company stage (mature companies naturally slow)
  - Consider if deceleration is from an unsustainably high base
  - A 30% to 20% slowdown may be fine; 30% to 5% is more concerning

### 2. Sequential Revenue Check
- Count consecutive quarters of QoQ revenue decline
- State clearly: "Revenue has [grown/declined] for X consecutive quarters"
- **Assess severity based on context:**
  - Consider seasonality (Q1 dips may be normal for some industries)
  - Consider macro environment (broad slowdowns vs company-specific)
  - 2 quarters could be a blip; 4+ quarters is a pattern

### 3. Margin Compression Check
- Compare current gross margin to same quarter last year
- State clearly: "Margins are [expanding/stable/compressing] - from X% to Y%"
- **Assess severity based on context:**
  - Consider if compression is from investment in growth
  - Consider competitive dynamics (price wars vs structural issues)
  - Consider one-time costs vs ongoing margin erosion

### Severity Scale (Use Your Judgment)
| Severity | Indicator | Typical Rating Impact |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Yellow Flag | Moderate change, worth monitoring | May still warrant Strong rating with explanation |
| Red Flag | Significant deterioration | Should impact rating; explain why BUY is still justified (if it is) |
| Critical | Severe, sustained deterioration | Likely warrants downgrade to HOLD or SELL |

### Rating Guidance (Flexible, Not Rigid)
- If you see concerning trends but still recommend BUY, **explain your reasoning**
- Recent momentum (last 2-4 quarters) should be weighted heavily, but not mechanically
- Multiple warning signs together are more concerning than any single metric
- Context matters: a cyclical company in a downturn is different from a growth company losing steam

## Writing Style Guidelines

**CRITICAL**: Write for someone who knows what a stock is but isn't a finance expert.

1. **Plain English First**: When you use financial terms, immediately explain them in simple language. VARY your explanations - never use the same analogy twice across reports. Be creative with how you break down concepts like:
   - Debt-to-equity ratio
   - Free cash flow
   - Operating margins
   - Interest coverage
   - Return on equity

2. **Use Fresh, Relatable Analogies**: Compare financial concepts to everyday situations, but ALWAYS create NEW analogies unique to this report. Draw from diverse contexts: personal finance, sports, cooking, gaming, streaming subscriptions, dating apps, road trips, etc. Make each report feel like a fresh conversation.

3. **Dynamic, Contextual Headers**: Create section titles that reflect THIS company's specific situation, not generic templates. Make headers catchy, informative, and specific to what the data reveals about {ticker}.

4. **Visual Indicators**: Use these for quick scanning:
   - Strong/positive: "+"
   - Concerning/weak: "-"
   - Neutral/mixed: "~"

5. **Conversational Tone**: Write like you're explaining to a smart friend over coffee, not presenting to a boardroom

6. **Standalone Reports**: Each report must be 100% self-contained. Users read reports independently, so:
   - NEVER reference other reports or previous analyses
   - NEVER use phrases like "as I mentioned before", "remember when I said", or "like we discussed"
   - Explain every concept fresh within THIS report - assume the reader has never seen another report
   - Treat this as the reader's FIRST and ONLY investment report they'll ever read

7. **Valuation Context**: You may comment on whether the stock appears cheap or expensive based on the historical financial data provided (EPS trends, margin expansion/contraction, growth trajectory). Use the 5-year data to provide valuation context rather than citing specific P/E multiples.

8. **Pairing Data with Varied Analogies**: When you spot concerning trends, show the technical data FOLLOWED BY a relatable analogy.

   Pattern: [Technical metric + change] -> [Relatable analogy]

   **VARY YOUR ANALOGIES** - You can reuse analogy TYPES (e.g., coffee shop, road trip, gym), but vary the DETAILS each time to keep reports fresh:

   GOOD - Same type, different details:
   - Report A: "...like a coffee shop that used to pocket $3 from your $5 latte, now only keeping $2.20"
   - Report B: "...like a coffee shop that raised prices but now has to offer discounts to compete"

   AVOID - Exact repetition:
   - Using the identical analogy word-for-word across multiple reports

   **Analogy sources** (mix it up based on what fits):
   - Everyday purchases & subscriptions
   - Sports & fitness
   - Road trips & transportation
   - Social media & streaming
   - Personal finance & budgeting
   - Gaming & entertainment

   **Rules:**
   - Avoid exact repetition of analogies in batch report generation
   - Vary the specific details even when using similar analogy types
   - Match the analogy to the magnitude of change (small dip vs major drop)
   - Keep it relatable to Gen Z/Millennial everyday experiences

   This gives financially-savvy users the hard numbers while helping newer investors understand what those numbers actually mean.
